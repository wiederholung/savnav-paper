\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

To navigate complex, dynamic human environments, a robot must do more than treat sound as a geometric goal. SAVNav processes social acoustic events as persistent social boundaries. The system operates through a three-stage pipeline: First, multi-modal perception fuses visual and acoustic streams into SAVMap, extracting both target locations and spatial social boundaries. Second, if the acoustic target is uncertain, the SAVNav policy queries SAVMap to find an optimal viewpoint for active sensory exploration. Finally, given an anchored target or a selected viewpoint, the policy generates a socially-compliant precision navigation path that explicitly yields to the modeled boundaries.

\subsection{Problem Formulation and Notation}
\label{subsec:problem_formulation}

We formalize the SAVNav task in a continuous 3D environment populated by dynamic human actors. Let $\mathcal{E}$ denote the environment, which contains a set of static obstacles $\mathcal{O}_{static}$ and a set of dynamic human actors $\mathcal{H} = \{H_1, H_2, \dots, H_N\}$. The robot is additionally given a static semantic map before navigation. This map provides four core priors: 1) a 2D navigable grid and obstacle distance field, 2) a set of precomputed topological nodes $\mathcal{N}_{topo}$ (e.g., corners, doorways, and corridor intersections), 3) the 3D spatial coordinates and oriented bounding boxes (OBB) of stationary semantic entities (e.g., doors, televisions, and sinks), and 4) pre-computed ImageBind cross-modal embeddings for these entities.

The robot state at time step $t$ is denoted by its pose in $\mathrm{SE}(3)$, from which we extract its 3D position $\mathbf{p}_{R}^{(t)} \in \mathbb{R}^3$. Each episode specifies a query token $q \in \{\texttt{doorbell}, \texttt{human call}, \texttt{television}, \texttt{water tap}\}$ that identifies the acoustic target. We define successful task completion as reaching a state where $\|\mathbf{p}_{R}^{(t)} - \mathbf{p}_{target}\| \le d_{success}$ without colliding with any human actor, where $\mathbf{p}_{target}$ is the ground-truth location of the acoustic target and $d_{success}$ is the distance threshold.

In addition to the acoustic target, human actors $H_i \in \mathcal{H}$ actively emit social acoustic events (e.g., \texttt{footsteps} and \texttt{conversation}). The robot does not observe the ground-truth states of $\mathcal{H}$ and must dynamically infer their presence and boundaries from egocentric sensory observations. At each time step $t$, the multimodal observation $O^{(t)} = (I^{(t)}, D^{(t)}, W^{(t)})$ consists of an RGB image $I^{(t)}$, an aligned depth map $D^{(t)}$, and an acoustic waveform $W^{(t)}$ captured immediately prior to $t$.

The objective is to compute velocity commands $\mathbf{v}^{(t)}$ that reach the acoustic target efficiently while avoiding collisions and social boundary violations. Table~\ref{tab:notation} summarizes the main notation used below.

\subsection{Multi-Modal Perception}
\label{subsec:perception}

The perception layer extracts spatial and semantic attributes from raw sensory streams and transforms them into object-centric representations.

\subsubsection{Visual Perception}
The visual module processes egocentric RGB-D observations using YOLO26~\cite{YOLO26KeyArchitectural2026} to perform real-time human detection and segmentation. For each detected mask, we extract the 3D position $\mathbf{p}_{vis} \in \mathbb{R}^3$ via depth projection. To quantify measurement uncertainty caused by distance and occlusion, we assign a spatial uncertainty $\sigma_{vis} = k_d z^2 + k_{occ} (1 - \gamma_{mask})$, where $z$ is depth and $\gamma_{mask}$ is the mask ratio. We then crop the masked image region and encode it with ImageBind~\cite{ImageBindOneEmbedding2023} to obtain a visual embedding in a joint embedding space. For static physical objects (e.g., doors, TVs, sinks) that may act as acoustic targets, their locations, bounding boxes, and pre-computed ImageBind semantic embeddings are retrieved directly from the static semantic map to be used in cross-modal comparisons.

\subsubsection{Acoustic Perception}
The acoustic module processes continuous FOA audio streams. Given the transient nature of sound, we adopt a sliding-window strategy, feeding short audio clips (e.g., $2.55\,\mathrm{s}$ duration) into the embed-ACCDOA model~\cite{OpenvocabularySoundEvent2025} at a fixed temporal stride. This yields the direction-of-arrival (DOA), denoted as angles $(\theta_{azi}, \theta_{ele})$ in the ego frame, together with a CLAP embedding~\cite{CLAPLearningAudio2023}. To classify the sound, we compute the cosine similarity between the extracted CLAP embedding and the text embeddings of all expected semantic labels (including both the query token $q$ and distractor social events). The label with the highest similarity is assigned to the acoustic event, and we then encode this text label with ImageBind. This transforms the acoustic detection into the same joint embedding space as the visual entities, enabling direct cross-modal comparison. The ego-centric DOA is transformed into a world-frame directional vector $\mathbf{d}_{world} \in \mathbb{R}^3$ using the robot pose at the event timestamp. To account for localization noise, we assign an angular uncertainty $\sigma_{ang}$ scaled by the model confidence.

\subsection{Acoustic-to-Spatial Social Mapping (SAVMap)}
\label{subsec:savmap}

Traditional social navigation relies on instantaneous LOS detections. SAVMap maintains three coupled states: anchoring confidence for LOS association, NLOS risk belief over topological nodes, and the social cost field used by the SAVNav policy.

\subsubsection{Audio-Visual Anchoring}
Acoustic DOA estimates are inherently noisy and lack depth information. To stably associate an acoustic event with its physical source, we perform audio-visual anchoring. This process targets two distinct entity types: 1) static mapped objects acting as acoustic targets (e.g., \texttt{doorbell}, \texttt{television}), where the visual location is known from the map but the acoustic activation must be verified; and 2) dynamic humans emitting social events (\texttt{conversation}, \texttt{footsteps}), which must be visually confirmed to establish a firm social boundary.

For each incoming acoustic event $E_{snd}$, we evaluate all candidate visual entities $E_i$ (either tracked LOS humans or static objects, depending on the event label) inside the DOA cone defined by $\sigma_{ang}$. While the ImageBind similarity $s$ provides a semantic baseline, it may not uniquely identify the source when multiple entities of the same class occupy the cone. The anchoring mechanism resolves this ambiguity by weighting the semantic match against the angular deviation from the DOA. The anchoring confidence is updated as
\begin{equation}
    S_{anch}^{(t)}(E_i) = \min \left( 1, S_{anch}^{(t-1)}(E_i) + \alpha \cdot s \cdot \exp\left(-\frac{\Delta\theta_i^2}{2\sigma_{ang}^2}\right) \right)
    \label{eq:anch_update}
\end{equation}
where $s$ is the ImageBind cosine similarity, $\Delta\theta_i$ is the angular deviation of $E_i$ from the DOA direction, and $\alpha$ is the update step size. Entities outside the acoustic cone undergo exponential decay. To prevent erratic anchoring state toggling, we apply hysteresis with an upper threshold $\tau_{anch}$ for engagement and a lower threshold $\tau_{deanch}$ for disengagement. Once anchored, the acoustic event inherits the 3D position and spatial uncertainty of the corresponding visual entity.

\subsubsection{Topology-Aware Acoustic Anticipation}
When a social acoustic event remains unanchored to any LOS visual entity, SAVMap predicts NLOS risk on topological nodes rather than on free space.

Given an unanchored acoustic event $E_{snd}$ with class $c_A$, world-frame direction $\mathbf{d}_{world}$, and angular uncertainty $\sigma_{ang}$, we first construct a candidate set
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{N}_{cand}^{(t)} = \{ n_j \in \mathcal{N}_{topo} \mid \Delta\theta_j < \tau_{cone} \}
    \label{eq:cand_set}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{N}_{topo}$ is the set of precomputed topological nodes, $\Delta\theta_j = \phi(n_j, \mathbf{d}_{world})$ is the angular deviation (in radians) between the node's direction and the DOA, and $\tau_{cone}$ is the acoustic cone boundary. This filtering retains only acoustically plausible topological exits.

In indoor scenes, diffraction makes corners and doorways act as secondary sound emitters, so the measured DOA often aligns with the node through which sound energy emerges. Instead of treating the DOA ray as a strict geometric vector, we evaluate the plausibility of each candidate node.

Each candidate node $n_j$ is assigned a heuristic risk score
\begin{equation}
    S_{nlos}^{(t)}(n_j) = \lambda S_{nlos}^{(t-1)}(n_j) + (1-\lambda) \exp\left(-\frac{\Delta\theta_j^2}{2\sigma_{ang}^2}\right)
    \label{eq:nlos_score}
\end{equation}
where the exponential term measures directional alignment and $\lambda$ controls temporal smoothing to reject transient SELD noise. For nodes outside $\mathcal{N}_{cand}^{(t)}$, the score simply decays by $\lambda$. Because the event $E_{snd}$ is unanchored (meaning no matching visual entity was found in the current line of sight), nodes within the DOA cone are conservatively treated as plausible emergence locations regardless of whether the node's exact geometric coordinate is currently visible. This continuous score bounded in $[0,1]$ circumvents the brittleness of single-point raycasting by maintaining a persistent, topology-aware NLOS risk belief over multiple emergence candidates. Crucially, this risk representation is strictly tied to the unanchored state of the acoustic event: if $E_{snd}$ ceases, the score decays exponentially to zero; conversely, if the human source enters LOS and becomes anchored to a visual entity, the associated NLOS scores are immediately cleared, seamlessly transferring the social boundary from inferred nodes to the observed entity.

For \texttt{footsteps}, the robot assigns a conservative velocity hypothesis to each node where $S_{nlos}^{(t)}(n_j) > \tau_{nlos}$:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{v}_{j} = v_{walk} \cdot \frac{\mathbf{p}_{R} - \mathbf{p}_{n_j}}{\|\mathbf{p}_{R} - \mathbf{p}_{n_j}\|}
    \label{eq:vel_hyp}
\end{equation}
where $v_{walk}$ is a reference walking speed, $\mathbf{p}_{R}$ is the current robot position, and $\tau_{nlos}$ is the risk threshold for assigning velocity.While a pedestrian emerging from a corner may theoretically follow the corridor axis, imposing a velocity hypothesis that points directly toward the robot serves as a robust, worst-case anticipation. This configuration enforces the most stringent frontal yielding constraint within the arrival-time field, ensuring the robot clears adequate braking distance for potential head-on conflicts, even if the eventual LOS trajectory deviates. For \texttt{conversation}, the corresponding active nodes are treated as stationary social boundaries.

\subsubsection{Social Cost Fields}
SAVMap converts both inferred NLOS risks and visually confirmed LOS entities into a continuous social cost field $C_{soc}(\mathbf{x}) \in [0, 1]$, defined as the element-wise maximum of its constituent components to prevent unnatural cost accumulation. We formulate this generically over a spatial probability distribution, where NLOS boundaries are represented as multi-modal risk fields driven by topological inference, and LOS boundaries naturally emerge as a deterministic special case.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textit{SCGs:} For NLOS \texttt{conversation}, we use a weighted mixture of isotropic Gaussians over the inferred candidate nodes:
          \begin{equation}
              C_{scg}(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{n_j \in \mathcal{N}_{cand}^{(t)}} S_{nlos}^{(t)}(n_j) \cdot A \exp\left(-\frac{\|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{p}_{n_j}\|^2}{2\sigma^2_{scg}}\right)
              \label{eq:scg_cost}
          \end{equation}
          where $A$ is the shared base cost amplitude, and $\sigma_{scg}$ defines the physical spatial radius characterizing stationary conversational groups.
    \item \textit{DPs:} For NLOS \texttt{footsteps}, we aggregate anisotropic fields induced by the candidate nodes and their corresponding velocity hypotheses:
          \begin{equation}
              C_{dp}(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{n_j \in \mathcal{N}_{cand}^{(t)}} S_{nlos}^{(t)}(n_j) \cdot A \exp \left( - \frac{x_j^2}{2\sigma_{x,j}^2} - \frac{y_j^2}{2\sigma_y^2} \right)
              \label{eq:dp_cost}
          \end{equation}
          where $x_j$ and $y_j$ are the longitudinal and lateral projections of the distance vector $\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{p}_{n_j}$ onto the velocity axis $\mathbf{v}_{j}$. The lateral variance $\sigma_y$ is constant to maintain a safe passing distance, while the forward spread $\sigma_{x,j}$ scales proportionally with the anticipated motion magnitude $\|\mathbf{v}_j\|$ to account for required braking distance.
\end{enumerate}

When a human enters LOS and the social acoustic event is successfully anchored via $S_{anch}^{(t)}$, the spatial risk belief collapses deterministically to the single visually confirmed entity, and the spatial uncertainty converges to the visual uncertainty $\sigma_{vis}$. Similarly, for dynamic pedestrians, the assumed worst-case frontal velocity $\mathbf{v}_j$ is replaced by the true velocity $\mathbf{v}$ estimated from visual tracking. The summations in the above equations thus gracefully degrade into single deterministic social costs centered at $\mathbf{p}_{vis}$. This unified formulation ensures that NLOS costs act as conservative acoustic anticipations, which seamlessly harden into precise visual LOS boundaries upon observation.

\subsection{Proactive Socially-Aware Navigation Policy (SAVNav policy)}
\label{subsec:savnav}

The SAVNav policy translates SAVMap into safe, target-driven motion. It operates on two map-level representations: the target spatial belief map for acoustic target search and the social cost field for social compliance.

\subsubsection{Active Sensory Exploration}
If the acoustic target is detected but remains unanchored due to occlusion or reverberation, the robot enters active sensory exploration. We maintain a target spatial belief map $\mathcal{B}(\mathbf{x})$ over the navigable space, which functions as an evidence grid storing log-odds probabilities of the target's presence. Following standard Bayesian mapping semantics, $\mathcal{B}$ receives positive log-odds updates inside the acoustic DOA cone upon target detection, and receives negative log-odds decay in any free space cleared by the robot's visual frustum.

To resolve target ambiguity, the robot samples candidate viewpoints $\mathcal{P} = \{\mathbf{p}_1, \dots, \mathbf{p}_N\}$ near topological nodes inside the DOA cone. The optimal viewpoint $\mathbf{p}^*$ is selected by maximizing
\begin{equation}
    U(\mathbf{p}_i) = \sum_{\mathbf{c} \in \mathcal{S}(\mathbf{p}_i)} \mathcal{B}(\mathbf{c}) \gamma(\|\mathbf{c} - \mathbf{p}_i\|) - \beta \frac{L(\mathbf{p}_{R}, \mathbf{p}_i)}{v_{avg}}
    \label{eq:utility}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{S}(\mathbf{p}_i)$ defines the unoccluded visible spatial region from viewpoint $\mathbf{p}_i$, $\mathbf{c}$ is a spatial coordinate within that region, and $\mathcal{B}(\mathbf{c})$ is mapped from log-odds to probability. A monotonically decreasing distance penalty $\gamma(\cdot)$ attenuates the utility of distant observations. $L(\cdot)$ is the geodesic path length, $v_{avg}$ is the nominal exploration speed, and $\beta$ balances information gain against travel time.

\subsubsection{Socially-Compliant Precision Navigation}
Once the acoustic target is anchored, or a clear viewpoint $\mathbf{p}^*$ is selected, the system generates socially compliant reference trajectories. We formulate global path planning as wave propagation in a heterogeneous medium, solving the Eikonal equation via the Fast Marching Method (FMM)~\cite{FastMarchingMethods1999}. The scalar speed field driving the propagation is
\begin{equation}
    S(\mathbf{x}) = S_{max} \cdot \left(1 - w_{soc} \cdot C_{soc}(\mathbf{x})\right) + S_{min},
    \label{eq:speed}
\end{equation}
where $S_{max}$ and $S_{min}$ denote the robot's physical speed limit and the minimum permissible speed, respectively, and $w_{soc} \in [0, 1]$ is the social sensitivity weight. Since $C_{soc} \in [0, 1]$, the field guarantees a strictly positive velocity lower-bounded by $S_{min}$, preventing path generation deadlock near dense social boundaries.The FMM intrinsically generates a local-minima-free arrival-time landscape. Because the continuous social field $C_{soc}(\mathbf{x})$ smoothly diminishes wave propagation speed near both LOS humans and high-risk NLOS nodes, gradient descent on the arrival-time landscape yields paths that naturally bend away from these boundaries early. This reference path is then passed to the robot's local motion controller to compute the final, kinematically feasible velocity commands $\mathbf{v}^{(t)}$.
