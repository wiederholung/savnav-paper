\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

To navigate complex, dynamic human environments, a robot must transcend treating sound as mere geometric goals. Our proposed system, Socially-Aware Audio-Visual Navigation (SAVNav), achieves this by processing ambient audio as persistent spatial social constraints. The architecture consists of three tightly coupled modules: Multi-Modal Perception, Acoustic-to-Spatial Social Mapping (\textit{SAVMap}), and Proactive Socially-Aware Navigation (\textit{SAVNav Policy}).

\subsection{Multi-Modal Perception}
\label{subsec:perception}

The perception layer extracts spatial and semantic attributes from raw sensory streams, transforming them into object-centric representations.

\textbf{Visual Perception.} Operating at $20\,\mathrm{Hz}$, the visual module processes RGB-D inputs using YOLOv11 for human detection. For each detected bounding box, we extract the 3D position $\mathbf{p}_{vis} \in \mathbb{R}^3$ via depth projection. To quantify measurement uncertainty caused by distance and occlusion, we assign a spatial uncertainty $\sigma_{vis} = k_d z^2 + k_{occ} (1 - \gamma_{mask})$, where $z$ is the depth, and $\gamma_{mask}$ represents the segmentation mask ratio. Semantic features are extracted using ImageBind~\cite{ImageBindOneEmbedding2023}.

\textbf{Acoustic Perception.} The acoustic module processes first-order Ambisonics audio streams. Given the transient nature of human sounds, we employ a sliding-window strategy, feeding $2.55\,\mathrm{s}$ audio clips into the Embed-ACCDOA model~\cite{OpenvocabularySoundEvent2025} every $0.5\,\mathrm{s}$. This yields the Direction of Arrival (DoA), denoted as angles $(\theta_{azi}, \theta_{ele})$ in the ego frame, and its associated CLAP semantic embedding. The ego-centric DoA is then transformed into a world-frame directional vector $\mathbf{d}_{world} \in \mathbb{R}^3$ by querying the robot's exact pose at the event timestamp. To account for localization noise, we assign an angular uncertainty $\sigma_{ang}$, which is dynamically scaled by the model's confidence output.

\subsection{Acoustic-to-Spatial Social Mapping (SAVMap)}
\label{subsec:savmap}

Traditional social navigation relies on instantaneous, line-of-sight detections. \textit{SAVMap} overcomes this by maintaining a spatio-temporal memory that anchors noisy acoustic vectors to physical geometry, ultimately generating dynamic social cost fields. 

\subsubsection{Sparse Belief Anchoring}
Acoustic DoA estimations are inherently noisy and lack depth information. To stably map audio events (e.g., footsteps, chatting) to physical entities (e.g., moving or static humans), we introduce a Sparse Belief Anchoring state machine. 

For each incoming acoustic event $A$, we evaluate its spatial and semantic consistency against all tracked visual entities within the DoA cone (defined by $\sigma_{ang}$). The belief score $S_{belief}$ for an entity $E_i$ is recursively updated:
\begin{equation}
    S_{belief}^{(t)}(E_i) = S_{belief}^{(t-1)}(E_i) + \alpha \cdot sim \cdot \exp\left(-\frac{\theta_{dev}^2}{2\sigma_{ang}^2}\right)
\end{equation}
where $sim$ is the cosine similarity between the acoustic and visual embeddings, $\theta_{dev}$ is the angular deviation from the DoA ray, and $\alpha$ is the update step size. Entities outside the cone experience exponential decay. A hysteresis mechanism (requiring $S_{belief} > \tau_{anchor}$ to anchor, and $S_{belief} < \tau_{deanchor}$ to release) suppresses erratic anchoring caused by single-frame SELD errors. Once anchored, the audio event inherits the 3D position and uncertainty of the visual entity.

\subsubsection{Topology-Aware Hallucinated Momentum}
The most critical challenge in socially-aware navigation is reacting to non-line-of-sight (NLOS) risks. When an acoustic event (e.g., unseen footsteps) remains \textit{unanchored} to any visual entity, SAVMap employs a topology-aware hallucination mechanism. 

Instead of treating the sound merely as an ambiguous directional cone, the system casts a ray along $\mathbf{d}_{world}$. Upon intersecting a physical wall, it queries the static semantic map to identify the nearest topological node (e.g., a corner or a doorway), denoted as $\mathbf{p}_{hallucinated}$. We then inject a virtual velocity vector $\mathbf{v}_{virt}$ pointing from the topological node toward the robot:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{v}_{virt} = v_{walk} \cdot \frac{\mathbf{p}_{robot} - \mathbf{p}_{hallucinated}}{\|\mathbf{p}_{robot} - \mathbf{p}_{hallucinated}\|}
\end{equation}
where $v_{walk}$ represents a standard human walking speed. This infers that an unseen pedestrian is likely approaching from the adjacent corridor, explicitly mapping acoustic cues to impending spatial interactions.

\subsubsection{Anisotropic Social Cost Fields}
To influence robot behavior, SAVMap translates both anchored and hallucinated entities into a continuous social cost map $C_{soc}(\mathbf{x}) \in [0, 1]$. We utilize dynamic, multi-shaped Gaussian fields to represent different social semantics:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Stationary Interactions (Chatting):} Modeled as isotropic Gaussians centered at $\mathbf{p}_{entity}$, ensuring the robot yields a polite radius around conversational groups.
    \item \textbf{Approaching Pedestrians (Footsteps):} Modeled as asymmetric anisotropic Gaussians. The spatial variance is elongated along the forward velocity vector ($\mathbf{v}$ for anchored entities, or $\mathbf{v}_{virt}$ for hallucinated NLOS risks):
    \begin{equation}
        C_{move}(\mathbf{x}) = A \cdot \exp \left( - \left( \frac{d_{long}^2}{2\sigma_{long}^2} + \frac{d_{lat}^2}{2\sigma_{base}^2} \right) \right)
    \end{equation}
    where $d_{long}$ and $d_{lat}$ are the longitudinal and lateral distances to the velocity vector. The forward spread $\sigma_{long}$ scales linearly with speed, effectively creating a protective ``spear'' that prevents the robot from crossing the pedestrian's anticipated path.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Proactive Socially-Aware Navigation (SAVNav)}
\label{subsec:savnav}

The \textit{SAVNav Policy} translates the rich spatial constraints of SAVMap into safe, target-driven motion. The planner operates by proactively yielding to social boundaries and, crucially, actively disambiguating target locations when acoustic evidence is uncertain.

\subsubsection{Active Sensory Exploration}
If the target acoustic signal (e.g., a call for help) is detected but remains unanchored due to severe occlusions or reverberation, the robot enters an active exploration state. We maintain a probabilistic target belief map $\mathcal{M}_{belief}(\mathbf{x})$, which receives positive updates within the acoustic DoA cone and negative updates in visually cleared, empty space.

To resolve target ambiguity, the robot samples candidate viewpoints $\mathcal{P} = \{p_1, \dots, p_N\}$ near topological nodes and within the DoA cone. The optimal viewpoint $p^*$ is selected by maximizing an information-theoretic utility function:
\begin{equation}
    U(p_i) = \sum_{\mathbf{c} \in \mathcal{S}(p_i)} \mathcal{M}_{belief}(\mathbf{c}) \cdot \gamma(\|\mathbf{c} - p_i\|) - \beta \cdot \frac{L(\mathbf{p}_{robot}, p_i)}{v_{avg}}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{S}(p_i)$ is the visible frustum area from viewpoint $p_i$, $\gamma(\cdot)$ penalizes excessive observation distance, $L(\cdot)$ computes the geodesic path length, and $\beta$ balances exploration gain against travel time. This ensures the robot does not blindly follow noisy audio vectors, but explicitly moves to ``look'' into ambiguous acoustic regions.

\subsubsection{Socially-Compliant Precision Navigation}
Once the target is anchored, or a clear viewpoint $p^*$ is selected, the system generates smooth, collision-free trajectories. We frame this as wave propagation in a heterogeneous medium, solving the Eikonal equation using the Fast Marching Method (FMM). The speed field $S(\mathbf{x})$ is defined inversely proportional to the social cost:
\begin{equation}
    S(\mathbf{x}) = S_{max} \cdot \left(1 - w_{soc} \cdot C_{soc}(\mathbf{x})\right) + S_{min}
\end{equation}
The inclusion of the base speed $S_{min}$ ensures that while the robot strongly prefers to route around social zones (e.g., chatting groups or the extended risk fields of unseen footsteps), it will not become permanently stuck if the environment is densely crowded. Gradient descent on the resulting arrival-time field produces proactive, socially compliant paths that naturally curve around both visible humans and anticipated blind-corner risks.
