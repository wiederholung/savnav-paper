\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

We evaluate SAVNav on the dataset introduced in Section~\ref{sec:dataset}. Our experiments are designed to validate three core claims: 1) safe navigation requires joint audio-visual perception; relying on vision alone leads to blind-spot collisions, while socially-unaware audio trackers violate social boundaries; 2) maintaining persistent spatial states (i.e., accumulating target and risk beliefs over time) is essential to stabilize yielding and handle occlusions, exposing the limits of instantaneous reactive methods; and 3) resolving severe occlusions and acoustic ambiguity specifically requires proactive reasoning, via topology-aware acoustic anticipation and active sensory exploration.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{subsec:exp-setup}

We evaluate all methods on the SAVNav dataset defined in Section~\ref{sec:dataset}, spanning the crowded social, hidden boundary, and mixed home activity scenarios. The navigating agent is instantiated as a Stretch~3 robot\footnote{\url{https://hello-robot.com/stretch-3-product}}, providing a forward-facing RGB-D camera and a 4-channel Ambisonic microphone array. Visual observations ($I^{(t)}, D^{(t)}$) are acquired at $20\,\mathrm{Hz}$, while acoustic waveforms ($A^{(t)}$) are captured over a $2.55\,\mathrm{s}$ sliding window. The robot's kinematic limits are bounded by $F_{\max}=0.5\,\mathrm{m/s}$ and $F_{\min}=0.05\,\mathrm{m/s}$, with nominal exploration speed $v_{\mathrm{avg}}=0.25\,\mathrm{m/s}$. We configure conservative anticipatory velocity hypotheses corresponding to typical human motion ($v_{\mathrm{walk}}=1.0\,\mathrm{m/s}$). All methods operate under identical episode constraints ($T=500$, $d_{\mathrm{success}}=1.0\,\mathrm{m}$) and are assessed via the dataset metrics.
% Due to space constraints, we omit the exhaustive list of empirical tuning constants (e.g., hysteresis thresholds, cost field variances, and decay rates). A complete specification of these hyperparameters is provided in our open-source repository at [ANONYMOUS_REPO_LINK].

We compare SAVNav against three baselines, each adapted to expose a specific capability gap:

\begin{enumerate}
	\item \textit{Falcon:} Adapted from Falcon~\cite{CognitionPrecognitionFutureaware2025}, this baseline performs purely vision-based human reasoning and never treats \texttt{conversation} or \texttt{footsteps} as NLOS cues, isolating the limits of LOS-only social intelligence. To decouple goal-finding from social safety, we evaluate two goal-source variants: \textit{Falcon+Goal Oracle}, which is handed ground-truth target coordinates, and \textit{Falcon+Audio Goal}, which instead consumes our audio-derived target anchor.
	\item \textit{ENMuS\textsuperscript{3}:} ENMuS\textsuperscript{3}~\cite{AudiovisualNavigationNoisy2025} is an audio-visual navigation framework that uses audio to pursue the target but ignores human-generated sounds as social boundaries. Because its released weights assume a fixed, largely single-source BeDAViN target vocabulary, we evaluate it on the ENMuS-compatible subset defined in Section~\ref{subsec:dataset-sim}; its numbers are therefore reported separately and are not directly comparable to the full-set methods.
	\item \textit{Reactive SAVNav:} This variant shares our exact multi-modal perception but disables all inter-frame accumulation ($\lambda_{\mathrm{nlos}}=0$ in~\eqref{eq:nlos_score}, $B_{\mathrm{anch}}^{(t-1)}=0$ in~\eqref{eq:anch_update}, and no $B^{(t)}(\mathbf{x})$ used by~\eqref{eq:utility}). It matches sound events to entities greedily per frame and projects waypoints at a fixed look-ahead $r_{\mathrm{react}}=2.0\,\mathrm{m}$ along the target DOA $\mathbf{d}_{\mathrm{aud}}^{(t)}$. It tests whether instantaneous multi-modal reactions are sufficient without persistent memory.
\end{enumerate}

Compared with the Falcon variants, SAVNav highlights the vulnerability of LOS-only human reasoning. Even when handed ground-truth target coordinates, Falcon+Goal Oracle cannot anticipate humans behind walls: in hidden boundary it incurs an NCR of 8.33\% (vs.\ our 5.65\%) and an HCR of 25.30\%, and in mixed home activity its HCR climbs to 38.99\%. Because a human collision terminates the episode, these frequent intrusions collapse its success rate to 13.99\% and 10.42\% respectively, far below our 37.50\% and 39.29\%. This failure is not a goal-finding artifact: even in the fully-LOS crowded scenario, where the oracle goal removes all target ambiguity, Falcon still collides in 30.95\% of episodes and reaches only 8.04\% SR, while replacing the oracle with our audio-derived anchor (Falcon+Audio Goal) degrades it further. SAVNav instead uses $\mathcal{N}_{\mathrm{topo}}$ to infer the most probable emergence node $n^*$ and its NLOS risk belief $B_{\mathrm{nlos}}^{(t)}(n^*)$ in~\eqref{eq:nlos_score} to yield in advance, cutting hidden-boundary NCR to 5.65\% and HCR to 14.29\%. This shows that visual forecasting alone, as instantiated by the Falcon variants, cannot safely manage blind-corner encounters regardless of goal knowledge.

Compared with ENMuS\textsuperscript{3}, SAVNav exposes the brittleness of socially-unaware AVNav. ENMuS\textsuperscript{3} is trained to isolate a single acoustic target from interfering noise and, confronted with our multi-source social scenes, fails to disentangle the target from concurrent human sounds, reaching 0\% SR across all three scenarios and making little spatial progress (Progress $\leq 18.1\%$). Its high PSC is a by-product of this failure rather than of compliance---never resolving the target, it wanders in open space and rarely approaches humans at all. By explicitly constructing the context-aware fields $C_{\mathrm{nlos}}(\mathbf{x})$ in~\eqref{eq:scg_cost} and~\eqref{eq:dp_cost}, aggregating them in~\eqref{eq:final_cost} before updating $F(\mathbf{x})$ in~\eqref{eq:speed}, SAVNav both reaches the target and maintains high social compliance (PSC near 95\%) across all settings, showing that treating sound as a social boundary---not merely a goal---is what makes acoustically-driven navigation both successful and safe.

Compared with Reactive SAVNav, we validate the necessity of persistent map-level states. Both methods share identical perception, yet stripping the accumulated target belief map $B^{(t)}(\mathbf{x})$ and anchoring confidence $B_{\mathrm{anch}}^{(t)}$ (see~\eqref{eq:utility} and~\eqref{eq:anch_update}) yields inefficient trajectories and delayed target resolution: SR falls to 23.21\%/22.62\%/25.00\% across the three scenarios (vs.\ our 37.80\%/37.50\%/39.29\%), with SPL and STL dropping in step. Notably, its HCR stays low---even the lowest in hidden boundary (13.39\%)---because it advances timidly; but this caution comes at the cost of task completion, confirming that persistent map-level state, not instantaneous multi-modal reaction alone, is what converts safe hesitation into successful, efficient navigation.

\subsection{Ablation Study}
\label{subsec:exp-ablation}

To isolate the contribution of each capability, we evaluate two ablated variants of SAVNav, shown in Table~\ref{tab:main_results}. Each variant removes one planner-relevant capability while keeping the rest of the system as fixed as possible, so that the performance drop can be attributed to a missing function rather than to a wholesale redesign.


\subsubsection{w/o Topology-Aware Acoustic Anticipation}
This variant ablates the spatial anticipation dimension by disabling the topology-aware cost entirely: it computes \emph{no} social cost from any NLOS sound event. Only sounds whose source is currently visible contribute to the cost field, so NLOS \texttt{footsteps} and \texttt{conversation} cues are ignored---the planner never extracts candidate emergence nodes from $\mathcal{N}_{\mathrm{topo}}$ nor evaluates $B_{\mathrm{nlos}}^{(t)}(n_j)$ in~\eqref{eq:nlos_score}. The agent thus reacts to occluded humans only once they enter view.

\textit{Results:} As expected, the effect concentrates in the NLOS-bearing scenarios. In hidden boundary, HCR rises from 14.29\% to 19.05\% and NCR from 5.65\% to 6.85\%, while SR drops from 37.50\% to 34.23\%; the degradation confirms that blind-corner safety requires reasoning about \emph{where} an NLOS risk will emerge rather than waiting for it to appear. In mixed home activity, where the LOS conversational group is usually visible, the gap in simulation narrows, though the full system remains ahead on every metric (e.g., HCR 13.39\% vs.\ 14.58\%). Crucially, this component proves decisive on the physical robot (Section~\ref{subsec:exp-realworld}): with reliable acoustic direction, removing NLOS audio cost causes a collision in \emph{every} occluded encounter.

\subsubsection{w/o Active Sensory Exploration}
This variant ablates the search dimension by removing information-theoretic utility evaluation. It does not maintain the target belief map $B^{(t)}(\mathbf{x})$ and disables $U^{(t)}(\mathbf{p}_i)$ in~\eqref{eq:utility}. If the acoustic target is unanchored, the robot executes a greedy pursuit: it bypasses viewpoint selection and assigns the nearest traversable point along the current target DOA $\mathbf{d}_{\mathrm{aud}}^{(t)}$ as its immediate waypoint.

\textit{Results:} This ablation primarily impacts target-reaching efficiency rather than social safety. SR drops from 37.50\% to 36.01\% in hidden boundary and from 39.29\% to 36.01\% in mixed home activity, with SPL and STL falling in step, while HCR remains largely unaffected. This pattern confirms that when targets are acoustically ambiguous due to occlusions, greedy directional tracking is less efficient than actively seeking information to resolve the ambiguity.

Fig.~\ref{fig:qualitative_results} presents one representative episode per scenario, exposing the internal states behind the aggregate metrics in Table~\ref{tab:main_results}.

In the crowded social episode, SAVNav anchors the \texttt{television} event to its static object by step 141 and enters socially-compliant navigation; the concurrent \texttt{conversation} and \texttt{footsteps} events raise cost fields around the conversational group and along the crossing pedestrian, so the planned path curves around both regions and the robot stops at the target at step 213. On the same episode, Falcon+Goal Oracle holds the ground-truth goal coordinates yet oscillates among the LOS humans near its start, and ENMuS\textsuperscript{3} never separates the target from the concurrent human sounds; both exhaust the episode without reaching the target (step 498).

In the hidden boundary episode, the \texttt{doorbell} propagates through several walls, and SAVNav anchors it to candidate door objects far beyond visual range (step 152), steering socially-compliant navigation down the corridor. At step 185, an unanchored \texttt{footsteps} event raises the NLOS risk belief in~\eqref{eq:nlos_score} at the topological nodes around the junction ahead; the anisotropic cost field in~\eqref{eq:dp_cost} covers the junction before the pedestrian enters view, and the planned path detours around it, reaching the doorbell at step 299. Facing the same junction, Falcon+Goal Oracle reacts only once the pedestrian becomes visible and collides at step 456; ENMuS\textsuperscript{3}, evaluated on its compatible subset, likewise collides with an NLOS pedestrian (step 186).

The mixed home activity episode exercises the full pipeline. At step 103, the \texttt{human call} is detected but remains unanchored, so the robot performs active sensory exploration: the target belief map accumulates probability inside the acoustic DOA cone, and the robot heads for the best viewpoint selected by~\eqref{eq:utility} while yielding to the conversational group's cost field. By step 182, continued audio-visual observation has resolved the target and the policy has switched to socially-compliant navigation; concurrently, \texttt{footsteps} from the NLOS dynamic pedestrian activate the topological nodes at the junction ahead, and the resulting cost field diverts the planned path moments before the pedestrian steps out. The robot passes the junction safely and stops beside the calling human at step 265. The two ablations fail on this same episode in complementary ways. Without active sensory exploration, the robot greedily chases the instantaneous DOA, repeatedly doubles back, and never resolves the occluded caller (not reached, step 398). Without topology-aware acoustic anticipation, the variant still detects the footsteps but assigns them no cost: at step 182---the very step at which SAVNav yields---its planned path crosses the junction just as the pedestrian emerges, causing a collision.

\subsection{Real-World Experiments}
\label{subsec:exp-realworld}

To assess whether SAVNav transfers beyond simulation, we deploy it on a physical Stretch~3 robot in a real indoor environment. The test space spans $6.50 \times 7.45\,\mathrm{m}$ ($48.43\,\mathrm{m}^2$), of which $33.95\,\mathrm{m}^2$ is traversable once furniture and static obstacles are excluded, leaving corridors and blind corners that reproduce the occluded topology exercised in the hidden boundary scenario.

\subsubsection{Setup}
The robot carries a head-mounted, vertically-oriented Intel RealSense D435if depth camera together with a ReSpeaker 4-channel Mic Array v2, which provide the RGB-D and 4-channel audio streams used for navigation. An additional external Intel RealSense D435i observes the scene to compute a ground-truth DOA for each active sound source. Acoustic targets and social sound events are rendered by portable loudspeakers that replay recordings drawn from the dataset audio pool, spanning the \texttt{television}, \texttt{doorbell}, \texttt{conversation}, and \texttt{footsteps} categories, with human actors playing the roles of the conversational group and the dynamic pedestrian. To keep the perception stack tractable on hardware while matching the simulation protocol as closely as possible, we simplify the auditory front-end relative to simulation: the on-board module performs only sound-event \emph{classification}, and the DOA is supplied as ground truth from the external camera rather than estimated by the SELD network.

We evaluate two methods---the full SAVNav system and its \emph{w/o Topology-Aware Acoustic Anticipation} ablation---under the three scenarios (crowded social, hidden boundary, and mixed home activity). We run 6 episodes per method and scenario, for a total of $2\times3\times6=36$ real-robot episodes, and report the same metrics as in simulation.

\subsubsection{Results}
Table~\ref{tab:real_results} reports the real-world outcomes. In the fully-LOS crowded scenario both methods reach the target equally often (SR $66.67\%$), and the gap is small because no occluded risks are present; nonetheless, SAVNav avoids all human collisions (HCR $0\%$) whereas the ablation already collides in one encounter (HCR $16.67\%$). The two NLOS-bearing scenarios expose the component starkly: without topology-aware acoustic anticipation the robot collides with the occluded human in \emph{every} episode (HCR $100\%$, SR $0\%$), since it only reacts once the human is already in view. SAVNav instead anticipates the emergence from approaching footsteps, still completing $50\%$ (hidden) and $33\%$ (mixed) of episodes while halving the collision rate to $50\%$. This mirrors and sharply amplifies the simulation trend: once the DOA is reliable, converting NLOS acoustic cues into a topology-aware cost field proved decisive for safety across these 36 episodes.
