\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\IEEEPARstart{N}{avigating} dynamic, human-populated environments remains a challenge in embodied AI and mobile robotics. A home or service robot must avoid physical collisions and respect implicit social norms. That includes keeping distance from a conversation, yielding to a human approaching from a blind corner, and responding to an acoustic target such as a doorbell or a call from a human. Humans routinely combine visual and acoustic cues in these settings, but current navigation systems usually process the modalities separately, which limits their ability to reason about social context.

Existing approaches to human-aware navigation largely fall into two lines, each exhibiting distinct failure modes in complex environments (see Fig.~\ref{fig:teaser}). Vision-based social navigation (SocialNav)~\cite{PrinciplesGuidelinesEvaluating2025, CognitionPrecognitionFutureaware2025, SocialNavTrainingHumanInspired2026, VLMsocialnavSociallyAware2025, ObstaclesEtiquetteRobot2026} relies on line-of-sight (LOS) visual sensors such as RGB-D or LiDAR to detect pedestrians and forecast trajectories. These systems cannot anticipate a human hidden behind a wall from footstep sounds, leading to blind-spot collisions (red dashed line), nor can they recognize a conversational group before it enters LOS. Audio-visual navigation (AVNav)~\cite{LookListenAct2020, SemanticAudiovisualNavigation2021, AVLENAudiovisuallanguageEmbodied2022, CatchMeIf2023, CAVENEmbodiedConversational2024, RILAReflectiveImaginative2024, AudiovisualNavigationNoisy2025}, by contrast, uses audio to locate sound sources, but usually treats sound solely as a target to reach or as background noise to ignore. It does not model social sound events as spatial social boundaries, leading to intrusions into ongoing conversations (blue dashed line).

To address this gap, we introduce the SAVNav task, an embodied navigation setting where an agent must locate an acoustic target while treating social sound events as spatial social boundaries. To solve this, we propose the SAVNav framework, which jointly reasons over visual and acoustic modalities to safely navigate around human-centric risks, whether they are in the line of sight or obscured by obstacles.

The core insight of SAVNav is to fuse spatial audio and visual semantics into a unified, continuous representation. Rather than treating sound merely as a geometric goal or reacting to humans only when they become visible, SAVNav grounds acoustic observations to tracked visual entities and utilizes scene topology to anticipate non-line-of-sight (NLOS) risks. This persistent spatial memory allows the robot to project both LOS pedestrians and inferred NLOS presence into a continuous social cost field. A proactive navigation policy then balances active sensory exploration---seeking informative viewpoints to resolve target ambiguity---with safe path planning to yield to these boundaries.

The main contributions are summarized as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textit{A Dataset for the SAVNav Task:} We define a task in which an embodied agent must locate an acoustic target while treating social sound events, such as conversations and footsteps, as spatial social boundaries. We support the task with a continuous simulation environment, multi-agent human dynamics, acoustic rendering, and social compliance metrics.
    \item \textit{Acoustic-to-Spatial Social Mapping (SAVMap):} We propose SAVMap, a spatio-temporal representation that turns human-generated sounds, including NLOS social sound events, into persistent spatial constraints. By combining audio observations, visual cues, and scene topology, SAVMap enables the robot to infer spatial social boundaries before visual confirmation.
    \item \textit{Proactive Socially-Aware Navigation Policy (SAVNav policy):} We design the SAVNav policy to translate SAVMap into safe, target-driven motion. It merges active sensory exploration to gather information about uncertain targets with precise motion planning to stay clear of inferred boundaries.
    \item \textit{Evaluation:} We compare SAVNav against representative vision-only, socially-unaware audio-visual, and memory-free reactive baselines, and further validate the system on a physical Stretch~3 robot. Experimental results across crowded, hidden-boundary, and mixed scenarios demonstrate that joint reasoning over visual and acoustic modalities improves safe navigation success. Ablation and baseline analyses further show that persistent spatial memory and topology-aware acoustic anticipation are critical for resolving NLOS social risks, while active sensory exploration improves the efficiency of resolving acoustic ambiguity.
\end{enumerate}
