\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\IEEEPARstart{N}{avigating} dynamic, human-populated environments remains a basic challenge in embodied AI and mobile robotics. A home or service robot must avoid physical collisions and respect implicit social norms. That includes keeping distance from a conversation, yielding to a human approaching from a blind corner, and responding to an acoustic target such as a doorbell or a call for help. Humans routinely combine visual and acoustic cues in these settings, but current navigation systems usually process the modalities separately, which limits their ability to reason about social context.

Existing approaches to human-aware navigation largely fall into two separate lines, each exhibiting distinct failure modes in complex environments (see Fig.~\ref{fig:teaser}). Vision-based social navigation (SocialNav) relies on line-of-sight (LOS) visual sensors such as RGB-D or LiDAR to detect pedestrians and forecast trajectories. These systems cannot anticipate a human hidden behind a wall from footstep sounds, leading to blind-spot collisions with a dynamic pedestrian (red dashed line), nor can they recognize a stationary group engaged in conversation before it enters LOS. Audio-visual navigation (AVNav), by contrast, uses binaural or multi-channel audio to locate sound sources, but usually treats audio as a target to reach or as background noise to ignore. It does not model social sound events as spatial social boundaries, blindly intruding into an ongoing conversation (blue dashed line).

To address this gap, we introduce SAVNav, a system for socially-aware audio-visual navigation. As shown by the green solid line in Fig.~\ref{fig:teaser}, SAVNav treats social sound events as spatial constraints rather than isolated signals, safely circumventing both the dynamic and stationary risks. The system is organized around perception, mapping, and planning.

In the perception module, we combine first-order Ambisonics (FOA) sound event localization and detection (SELD) via embed-ACCDOA~\cite{OpenvocabularySoundEvent2025} with RGB-D visual abstraction from YOLO26 and ImageBind to recover both the spatial location and semantic category of sound events and human activities. These streams are fused into SAVMap, a spatio-temporal representation that associates sounds with visual entities over time and anticipates unseen risks using the environment's layout. Given a pre-computed static semantic and topological map, SAVMap lets the system anticipate non-line-of-sight (NLOS) human presence from social sound events and project inferred entities into continuous social cost fields. The planning layer then uses the SAVNav policy to actively explore the environment when the acoustic target remains uncertain, and to execute socially-compliant navigation when social boundaries appear.

The main contributions are summarized as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textit{A Dataset for the SAVNav Task:} We define a task in which an embodied agent, provided with a static semantic and topological prior map, must locate an acoustic target while treating social sound events, such as conversations and footsteps, as spatial social boundaries. We support the task with a continuous simulation environment, multi-agent human dynamics, acoustic rendering, and social compliance metrics.
    \item \textit{Acoustic-to-Spatial Social Mapping (SAVMap):} We propose SAVMap, a spatio-temporal representation that turns human-generated sounds, including NLOS social sound events, into persistent spatial social constraints. By combining audio observations, visual cues, and scene topology, SAVMap enables the robot to infer NLOS social boundaries and dynamic pedestrians before visual confirmation.
    \item \textit{Proactive Socially-Aware Navigation Policy (SAVNav policy):} We design the SAVNav policy to translate SAVMap into safe, target-driven motion. It seamlessly merges active sensory exploration to gather information about uncertain targets with safe, precise motion planning to stay clear of inferred boundaries.
    \item \textit{Evaluation:} We evaluate SAVNav against representative vision-only, socially-unaware audio-visual, and memory-free reactive baselines. Experimental results across crowded, hidden-boundary, and mixed scenarios demonstrate that joint reasoning over visual and acoustic modalities is essential for safe navigation. Through an ablation analysis, we further prove that persistent spatial memory, layout-guided anticipation, and active information-seeking are strictly required to resolve NLOS social risks and acoustic ambiguity.
\end{enumerate}
